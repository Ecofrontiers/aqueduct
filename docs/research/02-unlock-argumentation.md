# Aqueduct — The Unlock Argument (evidence-backed, diligence-hardened)

> Research brief for the Sentient Foundation grant. Purpose: build the historical + economic
> case for what an open aggregation/certification/intent layer for smallholder commodities
> actually *unlocks* — the version that survives a hostile diligence panel. Every load-bearing
> claim carries a source. Claims we cannot ground are flagged as such, not laundered.
> Compiled 2026-07-02.

## TL;DR for the panel

The honest position is stronger than the marketing one. The historical record of
"transparency layers" for smallholder commodities is **mixed, not triumphant**: price-information
and exchange interventions move incomes only under specific conditions, and several flagship
efforts (Ethiopia's ECX, India's eNAM, African warehouse-receipt systems) delivered far less
for smallholders than their launch rhetoric promised — and in some cases were captured by
larger players. The funder's "30–60% lost to intermediaries" figure cannot be defended as
*pure extractable rent*; the best evidence says the recoverable information-rent at the farmgate
is more plausibly in the low double digits, and that most of the producer-to-consumer gap is
legitimate value-chain services. **Aqueduct's defensible thesis is therefore narrower and
more credible:** the unlock is not "delete the middleman and give farmers 30–60% back" — it is
"make the recoverable slice *actually recoverable* by pairing price legibility with the things
that RCTs show are necessary for price info to bite: an alternative buyer to switch to,
verified quality/origin so a distant buyer will believe the lot, and aggregation so a small lot
clears." Openness is the anti-capture mechanism, because the documented failure mode is the
new layer becoming the new rent-taker.

## 1. What happened when commodity transparency/exchange layers actually launched

### 1a. Ethiopia Commodity Exchange (ECX) — the cautionary flagship

- **Setup.** When ECX began trading coffee in December 2008, the government suspended the
  traditional Addis Ababa auction floor and made trading through the exchange **mandatory**.
  Coffee accounted for ~35% of Ethiopia's export revenue (2000–2014) and involved 4.2M+
  smallholder producers — so this was a high-stakes, whole-sector intervention.
  [IFPRI via Gustafson, "The Ethiopian Commodity Exchange: A Coffee Success Story?", foodsecurityportal.org, 2015 — https://ssa.foodsecurityportal.org/blog/ethiopian-commodity-exchange-coffee-success-story]
- **The headline claim.** ECX was "viewed as wildly successful," widely credited with linking
  **2.4 million smallholder farmers** to markets via cooperatives — a figure "heavily covered in
  both regional and global media." The IFPRI Discussion Paper's finding: that evidence was
  **"largely anecdotal; there has been very little systematic analysis"** of whether ECX actually
  drove the improvements. [same source]
- **The rigorous finding.** Examining coffee price dynamics Jan 1992–Jun 2013, ECX had a
  **limited impact on international-domestic price integration**: only **2 of 5** major
  coffee-producing regions saw increased interrelationships between auction and international
  prices; **no significant impact** on the spatial integration of coffee markets; **no
  significant change** in volatility transmission. [Hernandez et al., "Institutions and Market
  Integration: The Case of Coffee in the ECX," IFPRI, summarized in Gustafson 2015]
- **Why it underperformed (the panel-relevant lesson).** (i) A reasonably functioning
  centralized auction floor **already existed** in Addis, so the counterfactual was not chaos;
  (ii) ECX's strict regulations **raised transaction costs**, potentially cancelling its own
  innovations; (iii) structural constraints (weak infrastructure, low productivity) sat outside
  the exchange's reach. [same source]
- **Diligence read:** a *mandatory, state-run* exchange added cost and centralization without
  demonstrably improving smallholder price transmission. This is the strongest single argument
  *against* "just build an exchange" — and *for* an **open, voluntary, additive** layer that
  farmers/platforms opt into rather than are forced through.

### 1b. India eNAM — adoption breadth vs. real integration

- **Scale on paper.** By March 2026, eNAM reported **~1.8 crore (18M) farmers**, 2.73 lakh
  traders, and 4,724 FPOs registered. [Press Information Bureau, Govt. of India, PRID 2251543 —
  https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2251543]
- **Measured price benefit is modest.** A government review found eNAM-participant farmers
  received on the order of **~5.5% higher price realization**. [Dept. of Agriculture,
  "Electronic National Agricultural Market (e-NAM): A Review of Performance and Prospects,
  2020-21," desagri.gov.in —
  https://desagri.gov.in/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/2020-21-Electronic-National-Agricultural-Market-e-NAM-A-Review-of-Performance-and-Prospects.pdf]
- **The integration was largely notional.** eNAM is a virtual layer that **depends entirely on
  the existence of physical APMC mandis**; at first phase it networked ~585 mandis — **~9% of
  regulated wholesale mandis and ~2% of all rural market sites**. In "almost all cases only a
  single commodity" was taken up per market, on an optional basis, and small/marginal farmers
  continued selling predominantly to village-level traders.
  [Chatterjee & Krishnamurthy, "Understanding and misunderstanding e-NAM," *Seminar* 725, 2020 —
  https://www.india-seminar.com/2020/725/725_shoumitro_and_mekhala.htm]
- **Inter-state trade — the whole point — was legally blocked.** A government Expert Committee
  (2024) observed that inter-mandi and inter-state trade on eNAM faces **"legal obstacles under
  State" APMC acts**, i.e., the unified-market vision stalled on state political economy.
  [Expert Committee report on e-NAM, Dept. of Agriculture & Farmers Welfare, 5 Mar 2024 —
  https://agriwelfare.gov.in/Documents/HomeWhatsNew/ExpertCommittereport_eNAM_5Mar2024.pdf]
- **Diligence read:** a top-down national platform can rack up huge registration numbers while
  delivering a single-digit price bump and near-zero real market integration, because it presumed
  physical and legal infrastructure it did not control. Adoption ≠ unlock.

### 1c. African warehouse-receipt exchanges (ACE Malawi et al.) — who actually shows up

- **The promise.** Bilateral/multilateral agencies promoted WRS to stop smallholders
  dumping surplus at harvest-time low prices. [Thunde & Baulch, "Who Uses and Who Benefits From
  Warehouse Receipt Systems?", IFPRI MaSSP Working Paper 35, 2020 —
  https://massp.ifpri.info/files/2020/05/MaSSP-Working-Paper-35_Who-Uses-and-Who-Benefits-From-WRS_May-12-2020.pdf]
- **The reality.** "In practice, warehouse receipt financing **generally has not been embraced
  by smallholders** in developing countries… throughout most of Africa south of the Sahara,
  warehouse receipts are used **almost exclusively by large traders, processors, and exporters**"
  (citing Miranda et al. 2019). On the Agricultural Commodity Exchange for Africa (ACE, Malawi),
  2011–2018, depositors were predominantly firms making **large-volume deposits** (e.g. ~24 MT
  maize per multiple-depositor) — not smallholders. [same source]
- **Diligence read:** this is the "new middleman" failure mode *observed in the wild* — a
  transparency/credit rail whose benefits accrued to the already-powerful. It is exactly the risk
  the funder names, and the reason "open, value-stays-with-farmer" has to be a design constraint,
  not a slogan.

## 2. Warehouse-receipt financing: does verified-lot collateral actually unlock smallholder credit?

**Honest answer: the mechanism is sound but the beneficiary is usually not the smallholder.**

- The theoretical case is real: a graded, verified lot in a bonded warehouse is collateral a bank
  can lend against, letting a producer borrow at harvest and sell later. Development economists
  "have preached" this for decades. [Aboagye et al., "Structuring African Warehouse Receipt
  Systems to Succeed," *Athens Journal of Business & Economics*, 2022 —
  https://www.athensjournals.gr/business/2022-4930-AJBE-FIN-Aboagye-07.pdf]
- But the empirical record is that uptake among smallholders is thin and the users are large
  traders/processors/exporters (§1c; Miranda, Mulangu & Kemeze 2019). The binding constraints are
  minimum deposit volumes, storage/quality standards smallholders can't meet lot-by-lot, and the
  fact that a single smallholder lot is too small to be worth financing.
- **The Aqueduct-relevant inference:** verified-lot collateral unlocks credit **only when paired
  with aggregation** — pooling many smallholder lots into a financeable, graded, warehouse-held
  volume. This is precisely the aggregation-plus-certification function Aqueduct proposes, and it
  is the missing ingredient that separates the WRS successes from the failures. The credit unlock
  is *conditional on aggregation + credible grading*, not on the receipt alone.

> **Numbers caveat:** we did not locate a clean, generalizable RCT estimate of the *income*
> effect of smallholder WRS participation. Claims about WRS credit unlock should be stated as
> *mechanism + observed adoption pattern*, not as a quantified income lift.

## 3. Intermediation-rent economics: honest grounding for the "30–60%" figure

This is the claim most likely to get the application killed if stated loosely. Here is the
defensible version.

- **The measured farm share is low — but that is mostly services, not rent.** The best synthesis
  estimates the **farm share of consumer food expenditure averages ~27%** in middle-/high-income
  countries — i.e. ~73% goes to **value-chain services** (transport, aggregation, grading,
  processing, distribution, packaging, retailing), each of which must earn a return.
  [de Brauw, Maruyama & Wagner, "Is it a myth? Market Power among Intermediaries in Agri-Food
  Value Chains," CGIAR Rethinking Food Markets background paper, Dec 2024, citing Yi et al. 2021 —
  https://cgspace.cgiar.org/server/api/core/bitstreams/8f6e8663-51e3-4dc6-b6fc-74461d588379/content]
- **Crucially, we cannot rigorously measure how much of the gap is rent.** The same paper's
  headline finding: methodological challenges and poor data (FAOSTAT and multi-topic surveys are
  both "inappropriate" for this) mean it is **"not possible"** to say systematically whether — or
  how much — the producer-consumer gap can be reduced through fairer/more efficient chains. It
  names four distinct mechanisms behind low farmgate prices: quality-perception gaps, small
  volume, trade regulation, and market power/collusion — only the last is "rent." [same source]
- **Coffee, the anchor commodity, shows the low shares — and the trap.** Producers' share of
  total coffee value **fell from ~30% to under 10%** over two decades [Slob, "A fair share for
  smallholders," FAO — https://www.fao.org/3/at226e/at226e.pdf]; growers retain on the order of
  **~1% of a brewed retail cup's price** [Fairtrade International —
  https://www.fairtrade.net/en/get-involved/news/realistic-and-fair-prices-for-coffee-farmers-are-a-non-negotiabl.html];
  green-coffee export value is **<10%** of the ~$200–250bn retail coffee economy [Fromm,
  "Reducing Inequalities in the Coffee Value Chain," IntechOpen 2023, citing Coffee Barometer 2020 —
  https://www.intechopen.com/chapters/86940]. **But** the biggest slice of that gap is roasting,
  branding, and café retail in consuming countries — legitimate post-farmgate value-adding, *not*
  farmgate middleman rent. Conflating the two is the error to avoid.
- **Market power at the farmgate is real but bounded.** There is credible evidence of trader
  market power / incomplete pass-through (e.g. Sitko & Jayne 2014; Bergquist & Dinerstein 2020,
  cited in de Brauw et al. 2024), and international price *decreases* pass through to Ethiopian
  coffee farmers more strongly than increases — evidence of asymmetric, farmer-adverse
  transmission. [Kebede, "The Pass-Through of International Commodity Price Shocks," World Bank
  2022 — https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/bitstreams/59313ca0-3f3e-48f7-98e1-eed0464b49c7/download]

**Defensible framing of "30–60%":** state it as *"the share of a crop's final value that
smallholders do not capture,"* which is defensible and spans both services and rent — **never**
as *"lost to intermediaries"* as if it were pure extractable rent Aqueduct can hand back. The
extractable information-rent — the part a transparency layer can plausibly recover — is best
proxied by the RCT price-gain range in §4: roughly **0% to ~15%**, condition-dependent.

## 4. The RCT record on market-information interventions: what actually moved incomes

The literature is a natural experiment in *when price information bites*.

- **Kerala fisheries (positive, classic).** Mobile-phone adoption by fishermen produced a
  "dramatic reduction in price dispersion," near-elimination of waste, **fishermen's profits
  +8%**, and **consumer prices −4%**. [Jensen, "The Digital Provide," *QJE* 2007 —
  https://ideas.repec.org/a/oup/qjecon/v122y2007i3p879-924..html] — but the enabling conditions
  were extreme perishability and *spatial arbitrage across beach markets*.
- **Peru highlands (positive, RCT).** Farmers randomly given SMS price info received **13–14%
  higher prices**, with **larger effects for perishables and risk-averse households**, and became
  more likely to sell commercially. Effects were not driven by generic phone benefits (phones were
  SMS-price-only) or by production changes (intervention post-planting). [Nakasone, "The Role of
  Price Information in Agricultural Markets: Evidence from Rural Peru," PhD dissertation, U.
  Maryland, 2014 — https://drum.lib.umd.edu/bitstreams/e6166d21-81aa-4b61-a164-6d5101e1b093/download]
- **Reuters Market Light, India (null, RCT).** A 933-farmer / 100-village RCT with Thomson-Reuters
  found **no effect on prices, crop losses, or cultivation practices**; the only movement was in
  *where* farmers chose to sell. [Fafchamps & Minten 2012; J-PAL evaluation summary —
  https://www.povertyactionlab.org/evaluation/improving-price-negotiations-and-profits-through-sms-based-agricultural-information]
- **The synthesis (the actual insight).** Price information alone raised incomes where farmers
  could *act on it* — a perishable product forcing a sell decision, and a real alternative buyer/
  market to switch to (Kerala, Peru). Where markets were already reasonably integrated or farmers
  had no other buyer to switch to, information alone did **nothing** (RML India). What moved
  incomes beyond information was **the ability to reach an alternative buyer** — i.e. matching and
  market access, not the price signal in isolation.
- **Diligence read for Aqueduct:** this is the single most important design finding. A pure price
  oracle is an RML-style null result waiting to happen. Aqueduct's differentiator is that it pairs
  the price signal with the *complements the RCTs identify as necessary* — an intent/solver market
  that manufactures the alternative buyer, plus verified origin/quality so that distant buyer will
  transact. That is the mechanism by which price legibility converts into realized income.

## 5. Counter-arguments: where transparency layers failed or got captured

A panel will raise these. Pre-empting them is the credibility move.

1. **State-mandated exchanges can add cost without adding integration.** ECX: mandatory routing +
   higher transaction costs, limited measurable price-integration gain (§1a).
2. **Top-down platforms stall on political economy.** eNAM: single-digit price benefit, ~2% of
   market sites, inter-state trade legally blocked by the very APMC actors it needed (§1b).
3. **Credit/transparency rails get captured by the already-powerful.** African WRS: benefits
   accrued almost exclusively to large traders/processors/exporters, not smallholders (§1c, §2).
   This *is* the funder's "new middleman" failure mode, empirically observed.
4. **We can't even measure the rent reliably.** The systematic-data critique (de Brauw et al.
   2024) cuts *both* ways: it undermines loose "30–60% rent" claims, and it means the intervention
   must generate its own measurement (verified lots + settled prices) to prove its value (§3).
5. **Information without agency is inert.** RML India: transparency delivered to farmers who
   couldn't switch buyers changed nothing measurable (§4).

**How openness answers the capture critique (#3):** the differentiator is not "we're nicer" —
it's that an *open* protocol, where the data and the settlement layer are public goods rather than
a private book, structurally denies any single operator the ability to become the new
rent-extractor. Openness is the anti-capture mechanism, and the capture record is the evidence it
is needed.

## The five strongest sentences we can honestly write

1. When Ethiopia forced all coffee through its commodity exchange in 2008, rigorous analysis found
   it had a *limited* effect on domestic-international price integration — only 2 of 5 growing
   regions saw improved price linkage — a caution that building an exchange is not the same as
   helping farmers (Hernandez et al., IFPRI, via foodsecurityportal.org 2015).

2. India's eNAM registered ~18 million farmers yet delivered only about a 5.5% price improvement
   and reached roughly 2% of the country's market sites, because a virtual "one nation, one market"
   still depended on physical mandis and state laws it did not control (Govt. of India desagri
   review 2020-21; Chatterjee & Krishnamurthy, *Seminar* 2020; Expert Committee 2024).

3. Across Sub-Saharan Africa, warehouse-receipt financing "has not been embraced by smallholders"
   and is "used almost exclusively by large traders, processors, and exporters" — the exact
   new-middleman capture that an open layer exists to prevent (Thunde & Baulch, IFPRI 2020, citing
   Miranda et al. 2019).

4. In a randomized trial in rural Peru, farmers given SMS price information earned 13–14% higher
   prices — with the largest gains for perishable crops — showing price transparency *can* move
   income when farmers have a real alternative buyer to act on (Nakasone, 2014).

5. But an identical SMS-price trial with 933 Indian farmers found no effect on prices at all,
   proving that price information only pays off when it is paired with the ability to reach a
   different buyer — which is why an intent/matching layer, not a price feed alone, is the actual
   unlock (Fafchamps & Minten 2012; J-PAL).

## The three claims we must NOT make

1. **"Smallholders lose 30–60% of their crop's value to intermediaries."** Not defensible as
   extractable rent. The best evidence says most of the producer-consumer gap is legitimate
   value-chain services, and that the rent portion **cannot currently be measured systematically**
   (de Brauw et al., CGIAR 2024). Say "the share of final value smallholders do *not capture*,"
   never "lost to intermediaries."

2. **"A price oracle / market-transparency tool will raise smallholder incomes."** The RCT record
   includes a clean null (Reuters Market Light, India). Price information alone does *not* reliably
   raise incomes; only price information *plus a reachable alternative buyer* does.

3. **"Warehouse-receipt / verified-lot collateral will unlock credit for smallholders."** As
   built historically it unlocked credit mainly for large traders and exporters. Any credit-unlock
   claim must be conditioned on aggregation of smallholder lots — otherwise it repeats the
   documented capture failure (Thunde & Baulch 2020; Miranda et al. 2019).
